# community

Grow the open-source community around the project: stars, issues, and contributors, plus genuine engagement in the relevant maker and FOSS communities. For an OSS project, "community" is the repo and the people around it, not a Discord growth-hacking funnel.

Read `GROWTH.md` → Channels for where this project's audience gathers. The flywheel: users discover it → install → some open issues or PRs → the project visibly improves and stays alive → that activity drives more discovery and trust. Every action should feed that loop.

## The repo is the community home

Make it welcoming:

- **README that respects newcomers** — clear install, clear "build from source," clear "how to contribute" (wording via `copy`).
- **CONTRIBUTING.md** — how to build and run, the lint/test bar, the architecture rules (point to the project's own `CLAUDE.md` / `ARCHITECTURE.md` if present), and how to propose a feature vs file a bug.
- **Issue templates** — bug report (with version/platform fields) and feature request. Lowers friction to good reports.
- **Good first issues** — label small, well-scoped tasks. The single best lever for turning a star into a contributor.
- **Respond fast and kindly.** A maintainer who replies within a day or two, even just to triage, keeps a project feeling alive. Thank contributors by name; merge or explain.
- **Visible roadmap / changelog** — steady releases signal maintenance, which drives stars and trust more than any single launch.

## Earning stars honestly

Stars are the social-proof currency for a dev project. Earn them, never buy or beg crudely:

- Ship a genuinely useful, well-documented tool (the prerequisite).
- Ask naturally at the right moment: a "if this is useful, a star helps others find it" line in the README and release notes is fine. Never fake stars or run exchange schemes; it's detectable and corrosive.
- Get into the relevant `awesome-*` lists via honest PRs.
- Each release announcement (see `launch` / `social`) brings a wave; consistent shipping compounds them.

## Engaging outside the repo

Use the specific communities from `GROWTH.md`. In general:

- **Reddit (the project's niche subs)** — be a real member: answer questions in the topic even when they're not about your project. The 90/10 rule (90% genuinely helpful, 10% promotional) keeps you welcome.
- **Hacker News / Lobsters** — participate in threads about the project's domain. Technical credibility transfers to the project.
- **Mastodon (fosstodon)** — the FOSS crowd rewards open-source tools; share progress and help others.
- **Adjacent maintainers** — engage genuinely with neighboring projects. Maker niches are small and reciprocal; respect, not rivalry.

## Turning users into advocates

- When someone posts a positive review or a cool use, thank them and (with permission) amplify it.
- Credit contributors prominently in release notes and the README.
- If someone writes a post or makes a video about the project, link it from the repo.
- Translators are contributors too: if the project ships multiple languages, welcome and credit localization help.

## What to skip

- No paid community platform, no ambassador program with swag budgets, no partner/co-marketing deals. That's SaaS-scale machinery irrelevant to a free indie project.
- No engagement-farming or fake activity. For OSS, authenticity *is* the strategy.

## Health signals to watch

- Stars trend (steady > spiky).
- Issue response time and open/closed ratio.
- Number of distinct contributors.
- Recurring themes in issues (your roadmap and your `position` research).

## Deliverable

Per the ask: a repo-readiness checklist (templates, CONTRIBUTING, good-first-issues), a list of `awesome-*` lists and communities to engage (honestly, from `GROWTH.md`), or a plan to convert a launch spike into sustained contribution. Every recommendation honest and doable by a solo or small maintainer.
